# Slovak Language — Natural Marketing Copy Research

**Created:** 2026-05-07
**Last updated:** 2026-05-07
**Purpose:** Fix AI-generated Slovak copy before native speaker (Martina) review. Identify what sounds translated vs. naturally written.

---

## Key Findings

- For 25–39 events: **informal ty** is the right choice. Vy is formal/corporate. Ty is warm, direct, peer-to-peer.
- Slash notation (Skúšal/-a si) is technically correct but feels bureaucratic/inclusive-language-policy, not natural marketing copy. Better alternatives exist.
- "Benefit pochybnosti" is a calque from English legal usage. Not how Slovaks would say it naturally. Was right to replace.
- "Prihlás sa" is the correct imperative CTA button form. "Prihláš sa" would be a different (incorrectly applied) conjugation.
- AI-generated Slovak commonly mixes Czech/Polish patterns, applies wrong cases, and translates idioms word-for-word — all producing technically plausible but instantly detectable artificial text.
- Slovak sentence rhythm: short punchy sentences can work but need Slovak syntactic logic, not English structure translated word-for-word.

## 2a. Grammar Rules Specific to Marketing Slovak

### Formal (Vy) vs. Informal (ty) for 25–39 Events

**Rule:** Use **ty** (informal tykanie) for this audience and context.

**Evidence:**
- Slovak Spectator (native-language guide) states: the most basic rule is "if in doubt, vykať" — BUT this applies to strangers in general, not to marketing/events.
- For informal events (dating, social, sports, startup): small and informal settings normalize tykanie. Large corporate settings default to vykanie.
- The transition question "Môžeme si potykať?" is what happens when someone formally in vykanie wants to switch. That transition doesn't apply in copy written from the start — you set the register in the opening sentence.
- Dating events for 25–39 strangers who voluntarily come to meet each other = informal register. Using Vy would feel like a banking website or a government form.
- Czech speed dating (Rande Motýl, Prague) uses a warm, friendly tone. The equivalent Slovak register would be ty.

**Verdict:** Use ty throughout. "Skúšal/-a si", "máš", "chceš", "príď", "zaži". Not "Skúšali ste", "máte", "chcete."

[INTERPRETATION] No direct academic study on event marketing register in Slovakia found. This assessment is based on: Slovak Spectator guidelines, talkpal.ai Slovak formality guides, Czech competitor tone analysis, and analogical reasoning from informal event contexts.

### Gender-Neutral Constructions: Better Than Slash Notation

**Problem with slash notation:** "Skúšal/-a si" is grammatically valid but:
1. Signals policy-driven inclusive language effort (HR departments, government forms)
2. Disrupts reading flow — the eye has to process the slash as a choice point
3. Feels like a document, not a human talking to you
4. No native Slovak copywriter would write it in emotional/sales copy

**What Slovaks actually use in natural copy:**
1. **Rewrite to avoid gender entirely:** Instead of "Skúšal/-a si already scrollovanie?", use "Poznáš to pocit, keď..." (Do you know that feeling when...) — no gender needed.
2. **Use noun-based constructions:** "Každý, kto..." (Everyone who...) / "Ľudia, ktorí..." (People who...)
3. **Use plural:** Plural past tense doesn't mark gender: "Vždy keď sme skúšali..." (Whenever we tried...) — though this requires narrative restructuring.
4. **Lean into the universal "ty":** When addressing the reader as "ty," past tense does require gender. But marketing copy can often restructure the sentence: "Poznáš tých 200 matchov, kde nič nevyjde?" — no gendered verb needed.
5. **Use infinitive constructions:** "Scrollovať hodiny len pre mlčanie — to pozná každý." — gender-free.

**Sources consulted:** Talkpal.ai inclusive Slovak guide, Unbabel Slovak language guidelines (403 — inaccessible), Slovak grammar resources.

**Verdict on "Skúšal/-a si":** Replace with a rewrite that avoids the gendered past tense altogether, OR if the gendered form is retained for naturalness (e.g., "Skúšal si / Skúšala si" as two sentences), use one full form rather than slash. The slash is the weakest option.

### Common AI-Generated Slovak Mistakes

Based on Google Translate analysis and Slavic AI language research:

1. **Wrong case application:** Slovak has 6 cases with complex inflection. AI frequently applies the wrong case, especially in prepositional phrases. Example: "V aplikáciou" (wrong) instead of "V aplikácii" (correct).

2. **Czech/Polish bleed-through:** AI produces Slavic mixes. Example documented: "Potrebujesz jednoduchsi sposob" — mixing Polish "potrzebujesz" with Slovak "jednoduchší spôsob." Subtler versions appear in copy as words that exist in Slovak but feel Czech: "prostě" vs "jednoducho", "jenom" vs "len".

3. **English calques:** Direct translation of English idioms. "Benefit pochybnosti" is a textbook example. Others to watch for: "Na konci dňa" (calque of "at the end of the day"), "Urobiť rozdiel" (make a difference — works but signals translated thinking), "dáva zmysel" (makes sense — this one has actually been naturalized in Slovak, acceptable).

4. **Gender agreement failures:** Adjectives, participles, and pronouns must agree with noun gender. AI generates correct gender for explicitly mentioned nouns but fails when the subject is implied or contextual.

5. **Word-for-word structure from English:** English has Subject-Verb-Object rigidity. Slovak has flexible word order that encodes emphasis through position. AI produces grammatically correct but rhythmically flat Slovak because it follows English order. Example: "Inbox je plný" — correct but mechanical. "Plný inbox. Nič podstatné." — Slovak-rhythm version.

6. **Overuse of formal connectors:** AI uses "napriek tomu," "avšak," "v súvislosti s" (connectors borrowed from formal register) in casual copy. Natives would say "no," "ale," "lebo."

### Slovak Punctuation in Marketing

**What's standard:**
- Slovak uses **en dash (–)** not em dash (—). En dash has spaces around it. Em dash is a German/English convention.
- Slovak quotation marks: **„text"** (low-high). Not "text" or 'text'.
- Comma before subordinate clauses: obligatory in Slovak, more so than in English.
- Exclamation marks acceptable in marketing/events copy.
- Ellipsis (…) used in colloquial/emotional writing — acceptable in dating copy for tone.

**Implication for ČS Dating Wien copy:**
- If copy uses em dashes (—): replace with en dashes (–) or restructure.
- Short sentences and fragments are fine in Slovak marketing if rhythmically intentional.
- No strict "must be 16 words" rule for Slovak — sentence length is more flexible than English prescriptive advice.

## 2b. Natural Slovak Marketing Phrases — Register & Rhythm

**What makes Slovak copy feel native vs. translated:**

1. **Particle usage:** Slovak has discourse particles (veď, však, no, ale, lebo, len) that English doesn't have direct equivalents for. They carry tone. "Veď to poznáš" (You know how it is) feels natural. "Určite to poznáš" (You definitely know it) sounds translated.

2. **Clitic placement:** Reflexive particles "sa/si" have specific placement rules in Slovak. AI often gets them right but not always in conversational constructions.

3. **Colloquial vocabulary:** Slovak casual register uses words that aren't in the dictionary or look informal. "Scrollovanie" (scrolling) — fine and used. "Inbox" — fine. "Ghostovanie" — fine. "Bezproblémovo" — formal, avoid. "V pohode" — very natural.

4. **Rhythm:** Slovak likes noun-heavy, direct constructions. "Žiadne aplikácie. Žiadne texty. Len skutočný rozhovor." — This rhythm works in Slovak because the lack of verbs creates dramatic pause in a way that feels intentional, not incomplete.

5. **Warmth in events marketing:** Slovak event copy for young adults tends to be warm + direct, not corporate + polished. Reference point: Czech Rande Motýl site — warm, slightly playful, direct action verbs.

**What punchy short sentences need to work in Slovak:**
Hormozi-style short sentences CAN work in Slovak, but:
- They must follow Slovak syntactic logic (not just translated English)
- They need Slovak-specific connective tissue ("No.", "Lebo.", "Tak.")
- The rhythm must be earned — jumping from 3-word sentence to 3-word sentence works in English where the reader is conditioned to it; Slovak readers expect slightly more cadence variation
- Slovak marketing doesn't have a strong Hormozi-tradition — the style exists (see Slovak startup/SaaS copywriters) but is rarer and can feel imported if overdone

**[INTERPRETATION]** No Slovak copywriting study found. This is synthesized from: Slovak language resources, Rande Motýl tone analysis, Slovak startup marketing site patterns, and general Slavic language copy principles.

## 2c. Specific Words/Phrases — Verdict

### "benefit pochybnosti"
**Status:** [Confirmed unnatural — right to replace]
- "Benefit" is an English borrowing used in Slovak for "benefit" (as in employment benefit/výhoda), not for this legal/relational meaning.
- "Pochybnosť" (doubt) is correct Slovak.
- The phrase "benefit pochybnosti" is a direct calque from the English legal phrase "benefit of the doubt."
- Linguee examples show the natural Slovak renderings are: "preukázať dôveru" (demonstrate trust), "prikloniť sa vo váš prospech" (incline in your favor), or the EU legal term "privilégium pochýb."
- In a dating copy context, the natural Slovak expression would be something like: "veriť niekomu bez dôkazu" / "dôverovať zo začiatku" / "dať šancu bez toho, aby si vedel/-a."
**Verdict:** Was correct to replace. "Benefit pochybnosti" = instant translation flag.

---

### "Inbox je plný. Väčšina je hluk."
**Status:** [ACCEPTABLE — borderline]
- "Inbox" as a borrowing: widely used in Slovak tech/social media context. Acceptable.
- "Hluk" (noise) = correct Slovak word. However, this use of "hluk" as a metaphor for unwanted messages is a calque from English "noise" (signal/noise metaphor). In Slovak, "šum" or "zbytočnosti" might feel slightly more native.
- The sentence structure "Inbox je plný. Väčšina je hluk." is short and punchy — fine for copy.
- "Väčšina je hluk" — grammatically correct, but "väčšina je nezmysel" or "väčšina nestojí za nič" would feel more colloquial.
**Verdict:** Functional but could be sharpened. "Plný inbox. Nič zaujímavé." would feel more native. "Hluk" works but flags the translation origin to a careful reader.

---

### "Inštinkt" (used where a German speaker might say "Bauchgefühl")
**Status:** [ACCEPTABLE — elevated register]
- "Inštinkt" is a real Slovak word (from Latin, paralleled in most European languages).
- It is more formal/clinical than what a Slovak would say colloquially.
- **What Slovaks actually say for "gut feeling":** "pocit" (feeling), "tušenie" (sensing/intuiting), "cit" (sense/instinct in emotional context — e.g., "mám cit, že..."), or colloquially "niečo mi hovorí, že..." (something tells me that...).
- "Bauchgefühl" → Slovak equivalent in casual copy: **"čosi ti hovorí"** / **"vnútorný hlas"** / **"prvý pocit"**
**Verdict:** Replace "inštinkt" with "pocit" or "cit" for dating/emotional copy. "Inštinkt" works in sports/business copy but feels clinical in romantic context.

---

### "vtipkujúci v angličtine"
**Status:** [UNNATURAL — replace]
- "Vtipkujúci" is the present active participle of "vtipkovať" — grammatically valid but rarely used in spoken or casual written Slovak.
- Natives would say: "ktorý vtipkuje v angličtine" (who jokes in English) or "s kým sa vieš zasmiať po anglicky" (with whom you can laugh in English).
- The participial form (-júci/-iaci) reads as formal/written register — it appears in journalism and academic text, not in event marketing for young adults.
**Verdict:** Replace. "Vieš si s ním/ňou zažartovať po anglicky" is natural. "Vtipkujúci" is formally correct but sounds like a job description or a research paper.

---

### "odchádzajúci sami"
**Status:** [UNNATURAL — replace]
- Same issue as "vtipkujúci": the participial form (-júci/-iaci) used attributively sounds formal/literary.
- "Odchádzajúci sami" (departing alone) sounds like a headline about a political event, not dating copy.
- Natural alternatives: "odísť sám/sama" (to leave alone), "ísť domov sám" (go home alone), "skončiť večer sám" (end the evening alone).
- For copy: "Prídeš sám/-a. Odídeš s číslom." or just "Odchádzaš domov sám? Zmeň to." — natural.
**Verdict:** Replace participial construction with direct verb phrase.

---

### "Rezervuj si miesto"
**Status:** [ACCEPTABLE — natural]
- "Rezervovať si" is common and natural in Slovak event contexts.
- "Rezervuj si miesto" (Reserve your spot) — standard event marketing CTA. No issues.
- Alternatives that are also natural: "Zabezpeč si miesto", "Zabookuj si miesto" (even more casual/modern), "Kúp lístok" (Buy a ticket).
**Verdict:** Fine as is. "Rezervuj si miesto" is the right register for a paid event.

---

### "Prihláš sa" vs "Prihlásiť sa" vs "Prihlás sa"
**Status:** [RESOLVED — "Prihlás sa" is correct]

**Grammar analysis:**
- "Prihlásiť sa" = infinitive form. Used for dropdown labels, menu items ("Prihlásenie"), not for CTA buttons that address the user directly.
- "Prihláš sa" = This form is incorrect for a CTA. It would imply a 2nd-person singular present tense of a different verb conjugation pattern, or an incorrect imperative form.
- "Prihlás sa" = correct 2nd-person singular imperative of "prihlásiť sa." The imperative drops the "-iť" ending and follows the Slovak imperative rule for this verb class.

**Real-world evidence (confirmed on Slovak sites):**
- IQOS Slovakia login button: "Prihlás sa"
- ifortuna.sk: "Prihlás sa"
- e-blok.sk: "Prihlás sa"
- weleda.sk: "Prihlás sa"

**Verdict:** "Prihlás sa" is definitively correct. "Prihláš sa" is wrong (different verb/conjugation). "Prihlásiť sa" is the infinitive, appropriate for menus/forms not action buttons.

## 2d. Slovak Copywriting Conventions

### Do Short Punchy Sentences Work in Slovak?

**Short answer:** Yes, with Slovak-native syntax.

**Longer answer:**
- Slovak sentence structure is more flexible than English (free word order based on emphasis/topic).
- Short declarative sentences work when they follow Slovak logic: topic-comment structure, appropriate particles, natural prosody.
- Hormozi's exact style (English-native rhythm) transplanted word-for-word into Slovak will feel off because the rhythm of Slovak stress and sentence flow is different.
- Slovak readers in the 25–39 demographic ARE exposed to translated content constantly (Netflix, YouTube, brand advertising), so they are more tolerant of slightly non-native rhythm than older audiences.
- BUT a native Slovak speaker will immediately feel the difference between "written by someone who thinks in Slovak" vs. "translated with good vocabulary."

**What works:**
- Very short noun-phrases as sentences: "Žiadne swipy. Žiadne čakanie. Len ľudia." ✓
- Direct imperatives: "Príď. Porozprávaj sa. Odíď so číslom." ✓
- Rhetorical questions with colloquial vocabulary: "Koľkokrát si bol na prvom rande z appky?" ✓
- Repetition/anaphora: "Žiadne... Žiadne... Len..." ✓

**What doesn't work:**
- Calqued English rhythm: "Toto je rozdiel." (This is the difference.) — too blunt, reads like Google Translate
- Overuse of -júci/-iaci participles in punchy contexts
- English connector words smuggled in: "taktiež" (also — formal), "definitívne" (definitely — fine but formal)

### "Premium but Accessible" in Slovak — Vocabulary Signals

**What signals premium in Slovak:**
- Precision vocabulary (not generic): "Vybraní ľudia" (selected people) vs. "dobrí ľudia." "Starostlivo" (carefully) as adverb.
- Restraint: fewer adjectives, more nouns and verbs. Slovak associate overuse of adjectives with cheap marketing.
- Active voice, direct address.
- Numbers/specificity: "Maximálne 12 ľudí" signals curation.

**What signals accessible (not elitist):**
- "ty" register (not Vy)
- Colloquial vocabulary: "v pohode," "super," "skvelé"
- Humor signals: "Nikto ti nemusí vysvetľovať, čo je horalka." (Nobody has to explain what a horalka is.) — shared cultural reference = immediate in-group signal

**What signals cheap/generic (avoid):**
- Adjectives stacked: "skvelá, výnimočná, nezabudnuteľná príležitosť"
- Corporate connectors: "v rámci toho," "v súvislosti s," "napriek tomu"
- Passive voice: "bude vám umožnené" (you will be permitted)
- English phrases without integration: "fun" as standalone word, "vibe" without Slovak context

## Practical Checklist for Copy Review (Before Martina Sees It)

1. Replace all slash notation (word/-a forms) with restructured sentences
2. Confirm all CTAs use "Prihlás sa" not "Prihláš sa" or "Prihlásiť sa"
3. Check all participial modifiers (-júci/-iaci used as adjectives) — rewrite as clauses
4. Scan for English calques: "benefit X", "na konci dňa", "robiť rozdiel", "mať zmysel"
5. Confirm "Inštinkt" replaced with "pocit" or "cit" or "niečo ti hovorí"
6. Check register: every sentence with "Vy/vás/váš" form should be "ty/teba/tvoj" instead
7. Verify em dashes (—) replaced with en dashes (–) or restructured sentences
8. Check Slovak quotation marks: „text" not "text"
9. Rewrite "vtipkujúci" and "odchádzajúci" with direct verb phrases
10. Final gut check: read it aloud in Slovak rhythm — if you stumble, rewrite
